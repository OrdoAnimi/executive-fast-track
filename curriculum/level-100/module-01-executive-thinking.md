## Cool notes

### What this really means

Executives do not primarily reward depth. They reward clarity under pressure. The executive room wants to know what decision is needed, what changes if the decision is made, what risk is being accepted, what evidence supports the recommendation, and who owns the next move.

### Why executives care

A technically correct answer can still fail if it does not help the room make a decision. Executive thinking compresses complexity into action without pretending the complexity is gone.

### Words to steal

- Decision required.
- Trade-off.
- Execution confidence.
- Commercial consequence.
- Strategic risk.
- Accountable owner.

### Words to avoid

- Artefact maturity.
- Framework adoption.
- Capability taxonomy.
- Technical elegance.
- Best practice without context.

### How to say it plainly

"The issue is not whether the design is technically sound. The executive question is whether this decision gives us enough confidence to move, fund, and govern the work without creating unmanaged risk."

### One boardroom sentence

"The decision is not about the model; it is about whether we can move with confidence, evidence, and ownership."

## Feynman replay

Explain the difference between expert thinking and executive thinking in two minutes. Do not use the words framework, artefact, capability, or architecture.

## Optional Copilot drill

Tell the Copilot: "Run executive thinking drill. I will describe a technical idea. Convert it into decision, value, risk, evidence, and action."

## Scenario application

Take one current project or interview answer and rewrite it as an executive decision request. Start with the decision, not the background.
