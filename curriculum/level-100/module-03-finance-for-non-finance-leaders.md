## Cool notes

### What this really means

A CFO does not buy "better architecture" or "modern tooling." A CFO funds a credible investment case. The proposal must explain what money is required, what financial risk is reduced, what value is created, what waste is removed, and how spend will be controlled.

### Why executives care

Finance language is the trust bridge between technical work and business commitment. If the numbers are vague, the proposal sounds like aspiration. If the funding logic is clear, the room can decide.

### Words to steal

- Investment case.
- Payback period.
- Cost-to-serve.
- Margin impact.
- Opex versus capex.
- Risk-adjusted return.
- Funding sequence.

### Words to avoid

- It will be better.
- Modernisation value.
- Platform uplift.
- Strategic alignment without financial consequence.
- Efficiency without measurement.

### How to say it plainly

"The financial case is not that the platform is cleaner. The financial case is that this reduces value leakage, improves decision confidence, controls delivery spend, and gives us a staged funding path instead of one large blind commitment."

### One boardroom sentence

"The CFO does not need a better architecture story; they need a defensible investment story."

## Feynman replay

Explain your current product or consulting offer to a CFO in ninety seconds. Use cost, return, risk, funding, and evidence.

## Optional Copilot drill

Tell the Copilot: "Act as the CFO. Challenge my proposal on cost, return, funding sequence, and financial control."

Take one initiative and write a five-line finance frame: cost, return, risk reduced, funding stage, evidence required.
