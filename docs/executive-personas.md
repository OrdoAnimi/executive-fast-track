# Executive Personas

## Purpose

Executive Fast Track trains executive translation. The learner must be able to take one idea and rotate it through multiple executive lenses without sounding fake, generic, or over-technical.

Each persona is a working model, not a stereotype. The model helps the learner understand what each role values, fears, funds, blocks, and trusts.

## Persona template

Each executive lens should capture:

- Role mandate.
- Core values.
- What keeps them awake.
- What they fund.
- What they block.
- Evidence they trust.
- Language that lands.
- Language that fails.
- Typical objections.
- Strong response pattern.

## CEO lens

Mandate: Direction, growth, timing, execution confidence, reputation, capital, people, and strategic movement.

The CEO wants to know whether the organisation can move fast enough without breaking itself. They care about market window, execution risk, competitive pressure, strategic clarity, and whether the leadership team can align around action.

Language that lands: growth, timing, execution confidence, decision speed, market window, strategic risk, commercial movement, trade-off, consequence.

Language that fails: architecture maturity, artefact library, framework adoption, governance model detail before the decision is clear.

Strong response pattern: state the decision, why now, what changes commercially, what risk is accepted, and what action is required.

## CFO lens

Mandate: Capital allocation, margin, cash flow, cost control, investment confidence, financial risk, and commercial discipline.

The CFO wants to know why money should be committed now, what return is credible, what cost will be avoided, what risk is being funded, and whether the proposal has financial control.

Language that lands: investment case, cost-to-serve, margin impact, payback, opex, capex, risk-adjusted return, value leakage, financial control, funding sequence.

Language that fails: better platform, cleaner design, modern architecture, transformation ambition without measurable financial logic.

Strong response pattern: connect the proposal to spend control, risk reduction, investment sequencing, and measurable business value.

## CIO lens

Mandate: Technology strategy, enterprise platforms, resilience, delivery confidence, integration, security alignment, and business enablement.

The CIO wants to enable business speed without uncontrolled technical debt, cyber exposure, vendor lock-in, delivery fragmentation, or loss of enterprise control.

Language that lands: platform resilience, integration risk, enterprise control, delivery confidence, technical debt, operating model, roadmap discipline, business enablement.

Language that fails: isolated tooling, ungoverned pilots, architecture theory without execution, business promises without platform reality.

Strong response pattern: show how the proposal increases business pace while preserving control, resilience, and delivery credibility.

## COO lens

Mandate: Operating rhythm, execution, repeatability, process performance, accountability, handoffs, scale, and service delivery.

The COO wants to know how the work becomes repeatable, who owns what, where friction is removed, what breaks at scale, and how execution performance improves.

Language that lands: operating rhythm, throughput, handoff, friction, accountability, repeatability, execution cadence, process control, scalability.

Language that fails: strategy without operating mechanics, governance theatre, technology change without process adoption.

Strong response pattern: translate the proposal into repeatable execution, role clarity, operating cadence, and measurable performance improvement.

## CMO lens

Mandate: Market position, demand, brand trust, customer insight, differentiation, conversion, narrative, and growth campaigns.

The CMO wants to know why the market will care, how the message differentiates, whether the audience trusts it, and how demand converts into pipeline or adoption.

Language that lands: market narrative, differentiation, audience, trust, demand, conversion, category, positioning, customer journey, adoption.

Language that fails: internal capability language, framework detail, abstract value propositions, technical superiority with no buyer relevance.

Strong response pattern: show how the proposal creates a clearer market story, sharper differentiation, stronger trust, or better conversion.

## CISO lens

Mandate: Cyber resilience, exposure reduction, control maturity, incident readiness, identity, vulnerability, and board-visible security risk.

The CISO wants to know what exposure exists, how visible it is, what evidence supports prioritisation, what controls are working, and what happens after an incident.

Language that lands: exposure, control maturity, vulnerability, identity, incident readiness, audit evidence, threat surface, risk visibility, board reporting.

Language that fails: security architecture diagrams without risk consequence, compliance theatre, tooling-first answers.

Strong response pattern: connect the proposal to reduced exposure, clearer evidence, better prioritisation, and defensible executive decisions.

## CRO lens

Mandate: Enterprise risk, risk appetite, controls, assurance, regulatory posture, accountability, and evidence.

The CRO wants to know whether the organisation understands the risks it is accepting, whether controls are adequate, and whether leadership has evidence for decisions.

Language that lands: risk appetite, assurance, control environment, regulatory exposure, accountability, evidence, mitigation, residual risk, decision record.

Language that fails: speed without assurance, transformation without risk ownership, undocumented decision logic.

Strong response pattern: frame risk explicitly, define ownership, show evidence, and distinguish accepted risk from unmanaged risk.

## Chair and board lens

Mandate: Oversight, governance, CEO performance, strategic risk, capital stewardship, reputation, accountability, and stakeholder confidence.

The Chair and board want confidence that management understands the decision, options, trade-offs, risks, consequences, and accountabilities. They do not need operational detail unless it affects duty, exposure, or strategic confidence.

Language that lands: oversight, decision, options, trade-off, consequence, assurance, accountability, stakeholder confidence, governance position, strategic risk.

Language that fails: operational detail without decision clarity, technical explanation before board relevance, recommendations without clear ask.

Strong response pattern: present the decision required, options considered, recommended course, risk accepted, mitigation, evidence, and next governance checkpoint.
